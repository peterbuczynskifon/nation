Lay two white t-shirts side by side, one standard cotton and one Pima, and most people sort them by touch before they can say why. The Pima feels denser and smoother, with a faint coolness and a quiet sheen. It holds its shape against the palm instead of going limp. That difference is not a finish or a marketing tier. It comes down to the length of a single fibre.

Pima is one of the most misunderstood words on a clothing label. It gets used as a shorthand for "nice cotton" and quietly attached to garments that have little in common. So before it becomes a reason to spend more, it helps to know exactly what it is, where it grows, and what the word does, and does not, guarantee once it reaches a tee.

01 · What Pima Cotton Is A botanical fact, not a brand.

Pima is a variety of Gossypium barbadense, one of four cultivated cotton species and the one that grows the longest, finest fibres. Those individual fibres are called the staple, and staple length is the single number that explains almost everything else. Pima sits in the category textile people call extra-long-staple, or ELS: the small, top tier of cotton with fibres long enough to behave differently in the spinning mill and on your skin.

The everyday cotton in most t-shirts is upland cotton, a different species with short fibres around an inch long. Pima's run roughly half again to twice that length. Longer fibres mean fewer loose ends poking out of the finished yarn, and fewer ends is the root of every quality people associate with the fabric: a smoother hand, a thread that spins stronger and finer, a surface that resists pilling, and a fibre that takes dye deeply and holds it.

The short version

Pima is an extra-long-staple cotton with fibres up to twice the length of ordinary cotton.

That extra length is the whole story. It is why a well-made Pima tee feels softer, sheds fewer fuzzy ends, keeps its colour, and outlasts a cheaper one, not because the cotton was treated but because the fibre was longer to begin with.

02 · Where It Comes From One fibre, three homes.

The name traces back to the Pima people of the American Southwest, who helped cultivate the early extra-long-staple crop on experimental farms in Arizona in the first decades of the twentieth century. The plant itself, though, descends from cotton grown along the coast of South America for thousands of years. Today three origins matter most, and the country on the label tells you less than the variety does.

The United States. Pima grows in California, Arizona, Texas and New Mexico. When a garment is made entirely of American-grown Pima and certified through the trade body that licenses it, it can carry the name Supima, a trademark, not a separate plant. All Supima is Pima; not all Pima is Supima.

Peru. The Peruvian crop, grown in coastal river valleys and often hand-picked, is among the most prized in the world for its softness and consistency. It is the fibre behind a good deal of luxury knitwear, and the backbone of our own Peruvian cotton pieces.

Egypt and beyond. The celebrated Egyptian cottons belong to the same species as Pima, which is why the two feel like cousins. Pima is also grown in Australia and elsewhere. The takeaway: judge the fibre and the make, not the romance of a place name.

A coastal cotton field at harvest. Peruvian Pima is often picked by hand over several passes so only fully matured bolls enter the bale.

03 · What "100% Pima" Promises The fibre is half the answer.

Here is the part most guides skip. A label reading 100% Pima tells you what the cotton is. It says nothing about how that cotton was turned into cloth, and two tees, both honestly 100% Pima, can feel a world apart. The fibre sets the ceiling. The make decides how close you get to it.

Three things separate a good Pima tee from a wasted one. Combing removes the shortest fibres and lines up the long ones in parallel, which is why combed Pima feels clean rather than fuzzy; uncombed yarn throws away the advantage. Ring-spinning twists the fibres into a finer, stronger, softer thread than the cheaper open-end method. And weight, measured in grams per square metre, decides drape and longevity. A featherweight knit will feel thin no matter how fine the fibre.

So when you pick up an everyday cotton tee, the questions worth asking are simple: is it combed and ring-spun, and what does it weigh? A label that lists those alongside the fibre is telling you it has nothing to hide.

"You are not paying for the cotton. You are paying for the length of the fibre, and everything that length makes possible."

04 · How It Compares Same family, different questions.

Most of the confusion around Pima is really confusion about its neighbours. A quick map of where each one sits:

  • Pima vs. regular cotton. Standard upland cotton is shorter, rougher and cheaper; it pills sooner and loses shape faster. Pima costs several times more at the spool, and that cost is the reason most brands stop at upland.
  • Pima vs. Supima. Not a contest. Supima is the trademarked guarantee that a Pima garment is 100% American-grown and verified. The plant is the same. Supima just puts a paper trail behind it.
  • Pima vs. Egyptian. Both are the same species, both extra-long-staple at their best. "Egyptian cotton" is loosely policed as a term, so the named variety and the construction matter more than the word itself.
  • Pima vs. organic. A category error worth clearing up. Pima describes the fibre's length; organic cotton describes how the plant was grown. They answer different questions, they can be the same garment, and organic Pima exists.

05 · Is Pima Right For You A fibre for keeping, not churning.

Pima earns its place if you notice fabric and intend to keep your clothes. It is softer against the skin, it resists the surface fuzz that ages a cheaper tee within a season, it holds black as black and cream as clean, and it breathes well in heat. Spread over years of wear, the higher price quietly turns into a lower cost each time you put it on.

It is worth being clear-eyed, though. The word alone is not a promise. Pima knitted thin, spun cheaply, or skipped past the combing stage can still disappoint, which is exactly why the fibre, the yarn and the weight all have to line up. If you rotate through trend pieces every few months, the advantage is largely wasted on you. Pima rewards a wardrobe of fewer, better things.

If that is the wardrobe you are building, a single well-made piece is the easiest way to feel the difference for yourself. The Loren Tee is where most people start. Treat it simply: wash cold and inside out, only when it needs it, lay flat or tumble on low, and fold rather than hang so the knit keeps its shape.

A fibre that spent the better part of a year in the field tends to ask for a little patience in return. Give it that, and it stays with you long past the season you bought it in.
