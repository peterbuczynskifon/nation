What changed after nationltd.com → nation.la

Executive Summary

Each conclusion is triangulated across at least two datasets. Evidence = corroborated by ≥2 signals. Hypothesis = directionally supported, not yet provable with the data in hand.

  • 1
    The volume decline is real, not an attribution artifact. Shopify orders are down 30% and orders-fulfilled down 32% YoY — a hard ledger count, immune to attribution modeling — alongside net sales −33%. Because the 2025 base sits pre-migration, the drop is coincident with the migration year. Evidence
  • 2
    The technical migration was executed cleanly. Brand and homepage equity transferred (homepage clicks −7%) and product-page clicks grew +36%. Redirects and indexing held where it was hardest. The loss is narrow and specific, not a broken migration. Evidence
  • 3
    The one wound is the category layer. Collection pages lost ~53% of clicks and /collections/dresses fell 8,609 → 1,195 — yet that page still draws 155K impressions. The demand persists; what's missing is the content and link equity to capture it. Evidence
  • 4
    Non-brand is a visibility gap, and a content gap by design. Category terms impress heavily ("top" 33K, "puff sleeve top" 16K) but sit at positions 10–90. The technical fixes were delivered; the supporting editorial/content layer was pushed but historically constrained by brand choices — and is now greenlit. Evidence
  • 5
    Revenue is held up by owned + paid loops, which masks the gap. Email/retention (Klaviyo $866K + Repeat $792K) out-earns all paid combined, and Klaviyo is the only channel gaining efficiency (NVP +2.4). Meta was scaled in May (spend +38%, ROAS 2.3) to defend the top line. Evidence
  • 6
    The "Direct is down" read is partly a measurement effect. A large share of "Direct" sessions resolve to data-center cities (Ashburn, Council Bluffs +231%), and revenue re-bucketed from None·None (−39%) into None·nation (+76%) after migration. Hypothesis
  • 7
    Growth ceiling = organic non-brand acquisition. The fix is content + internal linking to collections. Retention is healthy (returning-customer rate 53%). The new topical-authority blog program, link-built down to the high-demand collection pages, is the highest-leverage move available. Evidence

Migration: Before → After

Drawn directly from the GSC footprint of each domain. This is a clean two-state comparison — legacy nationltd.com against current nation.la — not a speculative timeline. Every figure below is observed in the data.

Before

nationltd.com — legacy footprint
  • Brand terms ranked position 5.6; non-brand at 24.7
  • /collections/dresses was the #2 page site-wide — 8,609 clicks
  • Collection pages drove 19,232 clicks (the acquisition engine)
  • Homepage 13,774 clicks; products 6,187

After

nation.la — current footprint
  • Brand 12,746 homepage clicks (−7%) — equity transferred well
  • Products 8,434 clicks (+36%) — product layer recovered
  • Collections 8,983 clicks (−53%) — the category layer slipped

Reading the two states Where migrations usually break — brand transfer, homepage equity, product indexing — Nation's held or improved. The single area that did not fully carry over is the commercial category layer, which is the layer that converts non-brand search into new customers. That is a content-and-links problem, not a redirect problem, and it is squarely fixable. Evidence

04

SEO Structural Analysis

Comparing the legacy and current GSC footprints across brand/non-brand queries and page clusters isolates what the migration cost — and, importantly, what it did not. The conclusion points directly to the content roadmap in Section 08.

Credit where due The technical migration landed the hard parts: brand equity, homepage authority and product indexing all transferred. Historically the editorial/content layer that defends non-brand category rankings was proposed and pushed, but kept narrow by brand priorities. That constraint has now lifted — so the gap below is an opportunity that is finally actionable, not a fault line.

Brand vs non-brand — the dependency

93–96% of clicks come from brand terms on both domains.

The read Non-brand is just 4.5% → 6.6% of clicks, and non-brand average position moved 24.7 → 28.9. The brand is highly discoverable to people who know it, and close to invisible to those who don't. Closing that is the entire growth thesis. Evidence

Category authority & link equity

Contributing

SEO verdict The migration's SEO cost is concentrated in category authority and the content layer feeding it — not demand, not brand, not a broken migration. That is the most fixable kind of gap, and it explains why retention looks fine while new-customer growth stalls. Evidence

"Direct" session geography Hypothesis

Top "Direct" rows resolve to data-center cities, not consumer locations.

Bot-inflation hypothesis — read with care Ashburn (AWS) and Council Bluffs (Google data center, +231%) are infrastructure hubs, which is consistent with bot/proxy traffic rather than shoppers. This is a hypothesis, not a proven figure: geography is suggestive but not definitive, and some of these sessions will be real (VPNs, offices, crawlers that do convert). To confirm, we would isolate bot-flagged sessions in GA4 and compare conversion rate by city. Treated cautiously, it implies raw "Direct" session counts overstate human demand.

Two effects layered on "Direct"

Why the "direct is collapsing" read needs qualifying.

① Bot / data-center inflation Hypothesis

If a meaningful share of Direct sessions are non-human, they inflate the session denominator and depress apparent engagement — making the human experience look worse than it is. Needs GA4 bot-segmentation to size.

② Attribution re-bucketing Evidence

Post-migration, revenue moved out of None·None (−39%) into None·nation (+76%) — consistent with the same humans being re-labeled as the new domain's self-referral, rather than a pure demand swing.

What we can & cannot claim Reasonably supported: attribution re-bucketing shifted how Direct revenue is labeled (two signals: referrer revenue shift + the post-migration self-referral pattern). Not yet proven: a causal lag between remarketing spend and Direct sessions, and the precise scale of bot inflation. Both need a daily GA4 Direct-session series with bot filtering, aligned to daily remarketing spend. Until then, treat "Direct is collapsing" as a partial measurement effect layered on the real (~30%) volume decline.

07

Strategic Implications

What this means for growth — plainly, with precision retained.

What needs work

  • • Organic non-brand acquisition — category-page authority and rankings for product/category terms.
  • • Unit economics of paid — softening NVP, with discounts +21% and returns +15% buying volume.
  • • New-customer inflow — the ~30% order decline sits at the top of the funnel, not in the loyal base.

What is working

  • • Technical migration — brand + homepage equity transferred; product pages grew.
  • • Product-page visibility — recovered and grew (+36% clicks).
  • • Retention & email — RCR 53%; Klaviyo the only channel gaining efficiency.
The reframe This is not "the migration broke everything," and not "direct traffic collapsed." The migration's hard technical work succeeded; the missing piece is the content and internal-linking layer that feeds non-brand category acquisition — historically constrained, now unlocked. Rebuilding it with the topical-authority program re-opens the demand feeder the whole funnel relies on, and eases the margin pressure created by leaning on paid. That is the single highest-leverage move.
