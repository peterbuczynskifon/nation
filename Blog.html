The Quiet Power
of Pima.

Why a single cotton variety has become fashion's most considered material — and what its long, careful path from seed to stitch tells us about the future of premium basics.

There is a moment, when you put on a t-shirt made of properly grown long-staple Pima cotton, where you can feel the difference before you can name it. The fabric is heavier than you expected, denser, almost faintly cool against the skin. It does not feel papery. It does not feel like a garment you will be replacing in six months. It feels — and this is the word that returns whenever conversations about Pima begin in earnest — considered.

For the past two years, our team at Nation has been quietly rebuilding the foundations of our knit program around a single variety of cotton grown in the river valleys of northern Peru. The decision was not glamorous. It involved long flights, longer meetings, soil reports, and uncomfortable conversations about cost. But the case for Pima — for slowing down, for asking better questions about where our materials come from — has become, in this strange decade for fashion, almost impossible to argue against.

This is an attempt to explain why.

01 — The Material Question The fabric is the first ethical decision.

When most people think about sustainable fashion, they imagine recycled polyester windbreakers or reclaimed denim or a small, earnest cottage industry of vegetable-dyed linen. These are real things, and many of them are valuable. But they obscure a less photogenic truth: the single most consequential decision a clothing brand makes is not its dye process or its packaging or its return policy. It is the fibre itself.

Fibre selection determines water use, soil chemistry, farm labour conditions, manufacturing complexity, garment lifespan, end-of-life options, and roughly seventy percent of a product's lifetime carbon footprint. A growing body of research suggests that what we choose to make our clothes from matters more than nearly anything that happens after.

Why It Matters

Conventional cotton uses roughly 2,700 litres of water to grow the fibre for a single t-shirt.

That is more than most people drink in three years. Long-staple Pima, grown in regions with reliable river irrigation and lower pesticide loads, can dramatically reduce both the water and chemical footprint of a finished garment — provided it is grown carefully, and grown honestly.

The trouble, of course, is that "carefully" and "honestly" are not free. Pima costs roughly three to five times what conventional short-staple cotton costs at the spool. For most of the industry, that maths simply does not work. For a brand built around making fewer things, better, it begins to make sense.

02 — Why Pima, Why Now A slow renaissance for the right reasons.

Pima cotton is not new. It has been grown in some form along the coast of Peru for over five thousand years and was selectively bred into its modern form in the southwestern United States in the early twentieth century. What is new is the cultural conversation around it.

For decades, Pima existed as a quiet luxury — used by Italian shirtmakers, Japanese mill weavers, and a handful of American heritage labels. It was the fabric you discovered if you cared deeply about clothing, but it never broke through to mainstream attention. Three things have changed.

  1. Transparency tools have matured. Blockchain-tracked supply chains, third-party fibre audits, and DNA-level cotton verification mean it is finally possible to prove what your shirt is actually made of.
  2. Consumer attention has shifted toward longevity. The cost-per-wear conversation, once a niche preoccupation of menswear forums, is now part of how a generation thinks about its closet.
  3. The fast fashion model is structurally cracking. Regulatory pressure, returns logistics, and shifting consumer trust are forcing premium brands to differentiate not on novelty but on integrity.

In this environment, a fibre that lasts twice as long, feels markedly better, and can be traced back to a specific valley becomes something more than a material choice. It becomes a position.

03 — From Seed to Stitch A garment is the end of a long story.

The shirt you wear is the visible final chapter of a process that takes, in our case, somewhere between sixteen and twenty-two months. Understanding that timeline is the easiest way to understand why we no longer believe in seasonal drops.

The fibre takes its time

Pima plants are planted in late winter and harvested almost a full year later. Unlike fast-growing conventional cotton, the long-staple varieties require a specific combination of dry desert heat, mineral-rich soil, and steady irrigation from the Andes. They cannot be rushed. The plants set their bolls slowly, and the fibre cells inside lengthen over weeks. Harvest happens by hand, twice, to separate the fully mature bolls from those still developing.

A cotton field at first harvest, Piura Valley, Peru. Bolls are hand-selected over multiple passes to ensure only fully matured fibre enters the bale.

From bale to yarn

Once harvested, the cotton is graded, ginned to separate fibre from seed, and combed — a process that pulls out the shortest, weakest fibres and aligns the remaining long staples in parallel. Combing is the secret behind why Pima feels the way it does. Standard cotton is rarely combed because the process discards up to twenty percent of the harvested weight. For us, that loss is the point.

From yarn to cloth

The combed yarn travels to mills — some in Peru, some in Portugal, depending on the construction — where it is knit into the fabric weights our patternmakers have specified. Heavyweight jersey for our crewneck. Mid-weight rib for our long-sleeves. A finer interlock for our women's tees. The knitting itself is slow. A high-quality circular knit machine running heavyweight Pima will produce roughly a third of what the same machine would produce with cheaper short-staple yarn.

04 — The Cost-Per-Wear Argument The price tag is the beginning, not the end.

A Pima cotton crewneck made to our specifications retails for around three times what a fast-fashion equivalent costs. This is, on its face, an uncomfortable conversation. We have spent a great deal of time thinking about how to have it honestly.

The framework that has helped us most is cost-per-wear: the price of a garment divided by the number of times you actually put it on. By that measure, a $95 Pima tee worn 200 times costs you 48 cents per wear. A $30 supermarket tee worn 15 times before it pills, shrinks, twists, or simply stops being something you reach for costs you $2.00 per wear — and represents four times the environmental load.

  • Pilling resistance. Long-staple Pima resists the surface fuzz that destroys the appearance of cheaper knits within a season.
  • Dimensional stability. Combed, garment-washed fabric does not twist, shrink, or distort after washing.
  • Colour retention. The denser fibre structure holds dye more deeply, meaning blacks stay black and creams stay clean.
  • Repairable construction. Heavier-weight knits can be mended, darned, and tailored. Lightweight fast-fashion knits, generally, cannot.

None of this is a sales pitch. It is the accumulated knowledge of mills and tailors who have been working with this fibre, in some cases, for four generations. It is also why almost every serious clothing collector you can name has, somewhere in their wardrobe, a Pima cotton t-shirt they have owned for a decade and intend to own for another.

05 — Wearing With Intention A wardrobe is a relationship.

The deeper argument for Pima — and for the broader slow fashion movement it represents — is not really about cotton. It is about what kind of relationship you want with the objects that touch your skin every day.

Fast fashion taught a generation to treat clothing as disposable, almost edible: a fleeting mood, consumed and replaced. The alternative is not minimalism for its own sake. It is a different relationship — fewer things, chosen with more care, owned for longer, repaired when they break, and eventually passed on with their stories intact.

When you buy a Pima cotton garment, you are not just buying superior cotton. You are buying into a slower timeline. You are choosing a piece of clothing that was, somewhere along its journey, touched by people who took their time. And — if you take care of it — it will reward that decision for years.

A few suggestions, finally, for living with a garment like this:

  • Wash less. Cold water, inside out, and only when truly needed. Air between wears is your fabric's best friend.
  • Lay flat to dry whenever possible. Tumble dryers shorten the lifespan of even the finest knits by years.
  • Fold rather than hang. Knits stretch under their own weight on hangers; folded, they hold their shape indefinitely.
  • Mend small holes early. A single afternoon with a needle saves a garment that would otherwise be lost to the bin.
  • When it is finally finished — and good Pima takes a decade or more to finish — pass it on, donate it, or cut it down. It will still be useful.

That, in the end, is what we mean when we talk about conscious consumption. Not abstinence. Not minimalism as a lifestyle aesthetic. Just a quieter, more honest relationship with the things we wear.

The cotton is only the beginning.
